## Hidden in Plain Sight: A Museum of Cryptography

---

### Project Title
**Hidden in Plain Sight**

### Subtitle
A Museum of Cryptography

---

### Topic
The history and evolution of cryptography — from the earliest methods of concealing messages in ancient civilizations, through diplomatic and state ciphers, wartime machines, mathematical breakthroughs, and finally the invisible encryption that protects modern digital life.

---

### Main Thesis
Cryptography began as the art of hiding messages, but became the science of protecting trust. Across history, every new way to communicate created a new need for secrecy, and every new method of secrecy changed the balance of power.

---

### Audience
This site is designed for a general educated audience — curious people who are not necessarily experts in mathematics or computer science, but who are interested in history, power, communication, and ideas. A college student, a curious adult, or someone who just watched a documentary about codebreaking should all feel at home here.

---

### Purpose
The purpose of this project is to present the history of cryptography in a structured, curated, museum-style experience that moves the visitor from ancient origins to modern digital encryption. The site should feel like a real institution — organized, credible, and thoughtful — not like a report or a Wikipedia article.

This is also a demonstration of my ability to plan, design, and build a full web project from concept to presentation.

---

### Guided Visitor Experience
The visitor enters the museum and follows a guided route through five main exhibits, each representing a distinct era in the history of cryptography:

1. **The Birth of Secret Writing** — Ancient steganography and early substitution ciphers
2. **Ciphers, States, and Power** — The Renaissance, diplomacy, and the Vigenère cipher
3. **Machines of Secrecy** — WWI/WWII cipher machines, Enigma, and mechanical cryptanalysis
4. **The Mathematical Turn** — Claude Shannon, information theory, and public-key cryptography
5. **The Invisible Shield of Modern Life** — HTTPS, end-to-end encryption, and the cryptography you use every day

Each exhibit has a consistent structure: a framing statement, historical narrative, key figures or artifacts, and a closing idea that connects to the next room. The visitor always knows where they are and where they are going.

---

### Design Style
**Editorial archival modernism.**

The site should feel like a high-quality museum catalog crossed with a serious editorial publication — restrained, organized, typographically intentional, and historically grounded. It does not try to be flashy. It earns its authority through clarity and structure.

---

### Brand Archetype
**The Sage + The Explorer.**

The Sage provides credibility, depth, and intellectual seriousness. The Explorer provides forward momentum, curiosity, and the sense that there is always more to discover. Together, they create a site that teaches without lecturing and invites without overpromising.

---

### Cialdini Principle
**Curiosity Gap.**

The site uses strategic incomplete information — opening with a question or partial reveal — to draw the visitor forward into each exhibit and each page. Headlines, exhibit titles, and transitions are written to create a sense that the most interesting thing is always just ahead.

---

### What the Site Should Feel Like
- Walking through a quiet, well-curated museum
- Reading something written by someone who genuinely knows the subject
- A sense of forward motion and discovery, not obligation
- Trustworthy — the kind of site you would cite in a paper

---

### What the Site Must Avoid
- Sensationalism or exaggeration
- An overwhelming wall of text with no visual structure
- Fake depth — buzzwords without substance
- Generic web templates that look like every other school project
- Random facts presented without context or narrative
- Cluttered navigation with no clear path
- Visual gimmicks that distract from the content
