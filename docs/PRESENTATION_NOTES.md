# PRESENTATION_NOTES.md
## Hidden in Plain Sight — 5-Minute Presentation Outline

---

### Format
This is a rough outline for a 5-minute final project presentation. It is not a finished script. It is a map of what to cover, in what order, and what the key points are for each section. The actual delivery will be natural and conversational, not read from notes.

Estimated timing per section is approximate.

---

### Outline

---

#### 1. Opening Hook (~30 seconds)

Start with a question or a statement that creates immediate curiosity.

Possible opening: *"Every time you open a website, your browser and the server exchange a secret in less than a second — and neither of you had to meet beforehand to agree on it. That is the end result of 4,000 years of people trying to hide messages and other people trying to read them."*

Goal: Frame the thesis before naming it. Make the audience want to know more.

---

#### 2. Project Overview and Thesis (~45 seconds)

- Name the project: **Hidden in Plain Sight: A Museum of Cryptography**
- State the thesis: Cryptography began as the art of hiding messages, but became the science of protecting trust.
- Explain the scope: ancient origins through modern digital encryption
- Briefly state the format: a museum-style website with a guided exhibit route

Keep this short. The details come later.

---

#### 3. The Guided Route (~60 seconds)

Walk through the five exhibits briefly — one sentence each:

1. **The Birth of Secret Writing** — Where the first hidden messages came from and why
2. **Ciphers, States, and Power** — How secrecy became a tool of diplomacy and political control
3. **Machines of Secrecy** — The mechanical cipher machines of WWI and WWII
4. **The Mathematical Turn** — When cryptography left the physical world and became mathematics
5. **The Invisible Shield of Modern Life** — The encryption that runs underneath every digital interaction today

Explain that the route is linear by design — each exhibit sets up the next one.

---

#### 4. Design Decisions (~45 seconds)

- **Design style:** Editorial archival modernism — museum catalog crossed with serious journalism
- **Archetype:** Sage + Explorer — authoritative and curious, not dry and not sensational
- **Cialdini principle:** Curiosity Gap — exhibit openings raise questions before giving answers; every closing points forward
- Mention one specific design decision and why it was made (e.g., serif typography to signal historical depth, or whitespace to signal confidence)

---

#### 5. AI Process (~60 seconds)

Be honest and specific. Do not oversell or undersell the AI involvement.

Talking points:
- I used AI as a structured assistant, not a generator
- Every significant AI interaction was logged in the AI Orchestration Log
- I made the decisions — what to keep, what to revise, what direction to take
- Specific example: the initial project setup hit a technical error (npm naming restriction), and I worked through the workaround with AI assistance
- Specific example: the planning documents were generated with AI and then reviewed — I kept the thesis as written, condensed the exhibit route from six to five exhibits

The point is to show that AI use was intentional, traceable, and honest.

---

#### 6. Challenges and Improvements (~30 seconds)

What did not go perfectly, and what would I do differently:

- _(To be filled in after the project is complete)_
- Think about: scope decisions, technical challenges, content research, design trade-offs

Keep this honest. Professors appreciate genuine reflection more than a polished spin.

---

#### 7. Closing (~30 seconds)

End where you started — come back to the thesis or the opening hook.

Possible closing: *"Cryptography is one of those topics that sounds like it belongs to specialists — mathematicians, intelligence agencies, engineers. But it has always been about a very human problem: how do you trust someone you cannot see? That question has not changed in 4,000 years. Only the tools have."*

Invite questions.

---

### Notes to Self
- Do not read from slides
- Know the thesis cold — be able to say it in one sentence without looking
- Practice the exhibit route overview until it flows naturally
- Time the run-through before the presentation
- Have the site open and ready to show
